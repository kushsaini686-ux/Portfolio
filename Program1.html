A baby cow is called a calf, born after a nine-month gestation period and known for its wobbly legs and social, playful nature. Calves rely on their mother's colostrum, a nutrient-rich first milk, for essential immunity and are weaned onto solid foods like hay and grains around six to nine months of age. After weaning, they are known as weaners or yearlings, with female calves designated as heifers once they mature and produce their own offspring.

About Calf

"Calf" is the term used from birth to weaning, when it becomes known as a weaner or weaner calf, though in some areas the term "calf" may be used until the animal is a yearling.
The birth of a calf is known as calving. A calf that has lost its mother is an orphan calf, also known as a poddy or poddy-calf in British. Bobby calves are young calves which are to be slaughtered for human consumption. A vealer is a calf weighing less than about 330 kg (730 lb) which is at about eight to nine months of .
A young female calf from birth until she has had a calf of her own is called a heifers. In the American Old West, a motherless or small, runty calf was sometimes referred to as a dodie. Calves may be produced by natural means, or by artificial breeding using artificial insemination or embryo transfer. Calves are born after nine months. They usually stand within a few minutes of calving, and suckle within an hour. However, for the first few days they are not easily able to keep up with the rest of the herd, so young calves are often left hidden by their mothers, who visit them several times a day to suckle them. By a week old the calf is able to follow the mother all the time.
Some calves are ear tagged soon after birth, especially those that are stud cattle in order to correctly identify their dams (mothers), or in areas (such as the EU) where tagging is a legal requirement for cattle. Typically when the calves are about two months old they are branded, ear marked, castrated and vaccinated.
